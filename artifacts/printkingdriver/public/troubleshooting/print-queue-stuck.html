Print Queue Stuck — How to Clear and Reset It

When a job will not print and new jobs pile up behind it.

What Causes It

A print queue gets stuck when a job cannot finish — usually because the device was offline at the moment the job was sent, ran out of paper or had a driver mismatch. The job stays in the queue and blocks every job sent after it.

On PC the print queue is managed by the Print Spooler service, which runs in the background. On macOS the equivalent is CUPS (Common Unix Printing System). When the spooler hangs, the queue freezes until you intervene.

Step-by-Step Fix

Work through the steps in order. Most cases resolve at step two or three; the later steps cover stubborn problems.

  • Cancel every job in the queue — Open the device queue (Settings » Bluetooth & devices » Devices on PC, System Settings » Devices & Scanners on macOS), select each job and choose Cancel.
  • Restart the print service — On PC, open Services, find Print Spooler, right-click and choose Restart. On macOS, open Terminal and run sudo launchctl stop org.cups.cupsd then sudo launchctl start org.cups.cupsd.
  • Power-cycle the device — Switch the device off, wait 30 seconds, switch it on. Many devices also clear pending jobs from their internal memory at boot.
  • Check the connection — If the device is on Wi-Fi, confirm it is on the same network as the computer. If it is USB, try a different cable or port.
  • Re-add the driver — If the queue freezes again on the next job, the driver is the most likely cause. Remove the device from the system, reboot, and set up the latest driver from the manufacturer.

Why It Matters

Resolving this at the right level — driver, queue or hardware — saves you from chasing the same fault every few weeks. The wrong fix (for example re-adding the driver when the real problem is a stuck job) makes the issue look intermittent and harder to diagnose later.

When to Contact Support

If the problem returns within 24 hours of completing every step above, the underlying cause is likely a hardware fault — a worn pickup roller, a failing print head or a logic-board issue. At that point it is worth reaching out to the manufacturer's warranty support, or to our team for a second opinion before paying for a repair.
