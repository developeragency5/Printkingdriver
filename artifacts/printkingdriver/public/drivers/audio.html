Driver Reference

Audio Driver

Manages sound input and output processing.

What Is It?

An audio driver lets the operating system route digital sound to and from the audio hardware. It handles encoding, mixing, and signal processing so that microphones, speakers, and headphones behave consistently across applications.

How It Works

When an app plays a sound, the OS sends raw audio data to the audio driver. The driver mixes streams from multiple apps, applies any effects, and forwards the final signal to the audio codec. The codec converts it into an analog waveform sent to the speakers.

Key Functions

  • Mixes multiple audio streams from different applications.
  • Selects input and output devices and manages their levels.
  • Provides effects pipelines such as equalisation and noise reduction.
  • Synchronises audio with video to prevent lag.

Why It Matters

Audio drivers determine clarity, latency, and reliability. Without one, microphones go silent, speakers crackle, and meeting apps fail to detect any device at all.

Best Practices

A short checklist to keep this driver healthy and reduce the chance of running into the issues above.

  • Match the playback sample rate (44.1 / 48 kHz) across the OS, driver and app to avoid resampling artefacts.
  • Disable unused audio enhancements when troubleshooting — many crackle issues come from DSP plugins.
  • Keep both the audio codec driver (Realtek, Conexant, Cirrus) and any audio control panel in sync.
  • For external interfaces, use the manufacturer’s ASIO or low-latency driver rather than the generic Windows one.
  • Reboot after installing a new audio driver to fully release the old kernel module.

Frequently Asked Questions

Modern systems expose separate endpoints for HDMI, speakers, headphones and Bluetooth. Each is a logical device backed by the same driver. Set the default in Sound settings.

They are different audio paths. WASAPI is Windows native, DirectSound is legacy, ASIO is a low-latency standard for music production. The driver decides which paths your hardware exposes.

Most modern USB headsets are class-compliant — Windows installs a generic driver automatically. A vendor driver only adds extras like surround virtualisation or RGB control.

Calls use the HFP profile (16 kHz mono) so the mic can work. Music uses A2DP (stereo, higher quality). The driver switches profiles based on what the app requests.
