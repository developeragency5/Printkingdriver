Device Driver

Translates documents into device commands.

What Is It?

Device drivers act as translators between your computer and device, converting digital documents into a format that the device can understand. They control print settings such as layout, resolution, colour, and paper size.

These drivers ensure accurate and high-quality output, whether printing text documents or images. They also support advanced features like duplex printing and colour management.

Keeping device drivers updated ensures compatibility, reduces errors, and improves print quality.

How It Works

When a user prints, the OS captures the document and hands it to the driver. The driver renders each page into the device's native language (such as PCL or PostScript), sends it to the print spooler, and the spooler delivers it to the device over USB or the network.

Key Functions

  • Renders documents into a device-readable page description.
  • Manages paper size, orientation, duplex, and tray selection.
  • Communicates job status, errors, and ink or toner levels.
  • Queues multiple print jobs and supports cancellation.

Why It Matters

The device driver is the bridge between the digital document and the physical page. Issues here are the most common cause of garbled output, stuck queues, and offline devices.

Best Practices

A short checklist to keep this driver healthy and reduce the chance of running into the issues above.

  • Set Up the manufacturer’s full driver, not just the generic Microsoft IPP class driver, for full features.
  • For shared office devices, use the network host name rather than IP — it survives DHCP changes.
  • Clear the print spooler queue before re-adding a problem driver.
  • Match the colour profile of the device in the driver to the source document for accurate prints.
  • Keep the device’s firmware updated alongside the driver — they are often released as a pair.
