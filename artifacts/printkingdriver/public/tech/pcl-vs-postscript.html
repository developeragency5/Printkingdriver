Print Technology

PCL vs PostScript Print Drivers

A clear explanation of pcl vs postscript print drivers for non-engineers.

Quick Summary

PCL (Device Command Language) and PostScript are two device languages that tell the device how to render a document. PCL was developed by the manufacturer and is optimised for speed in office environments. PostScript, from Adobe, is device-independent and more accurate for complex graphics and professional design work. Most business devices support both.

  • PCL is faster for text-heavy office documents and is the default in most PC environments
  • PostScript is preferred for graphic design, professional publishing, and colour-accurate work
  • Both languages are interpreted by the device firmware — the document is rendered on the device, not the computer

Overview

PCL and PostScript are page-description languages — the language the print driver uses to tell the device what to draw on the page. Most office devices support both. Knowing the difference helps you pick the right driver for your workload.

What is a Page Description Language?

When you print, the driver does not send the device a bitmap of the page. It sends a description: "draw text X at position Y in font Z, then a rectangle from A to B in colour C". The device then renders that description into ink or toner. PCL and PostScript are two different ways of writing those descriptions.

PCL

PCL (Device Command Language) was developed by the manufacturer and is the dominant office device language. It is fast, lightweight and tightly bound to the host operating system's rendering capabilities. PCL relies on PC or macOS to do most of the heavy lifting before sending data to the device.

PostScript

PostScript was developed by Adobe and is the industry standard in design, publishing and high-end printing. PostScript is a full programming language — the device itself executes the description and renders the page. PostScript handles complex graphics, fonts and colour profiles more accurately than PCL.

Which One to Use

Use PCL for everyday office documents — Word, Excel, PowerPoint, web printouts. Use PostScript for design files — Adobe Illustrator, InDesign, complex PDFs. If your device supports both, set up both drivers and pick per job.

Speed Comparison

PCL is faster for plain text and simple graphics. PostScript is faster for complex graphics-heavy documents because the device renders them locally rather than receiving a huge bitmap from the computer.

Bottom Line

Office use: PCL. Design and publishing: PostScript. Mixed: set up both and choose per job.
