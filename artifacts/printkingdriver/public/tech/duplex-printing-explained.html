Print Technology

Duplex Printing Explained

A clear explanation of duplex printing explained for non-engineers.

Quick Summary

Duplex printing prints on both sides of a sheet of paper. An automatic duplexer flips the paper mechanically and feeds it back through the device for the second side. Manual duplex requires the user to flip and reload the paper halfway through the job. Duplex printing cuts paper consumption by up to half and is standard in most modern office devices.

  • Automatic duplex requires a device with a built-in duplexer — check the specification sheet
  • Long-edge binding is standard for portrait documents; short-edge binding is used for landscape and booklets
  • Enable duplex by default in the device properties to save paper automatically on every job

Overview

Duplex printing prints on both sides of the page. Auto-duplex needs hardware support; manual duplex is a workflow that works on any device.

Auto-Duplex Hardware

An auto-duplex device has a paper-path mechanism that flips the page after the first side is printed and sends it back through the device for the second side. Most office devices have it; many home models do not.

Manual Duplex Workflow

Print all odd-numbered pages first. Reload the printed stack with the printed side down (and the right edge as the leading edge — the exact orientation depends on the device). Print all even-numbered pages.

Long-Edge vs Short-Edge

Long-edge binding (book-style) is the default for portrait documents. Short-edge binding flips the back side upside-down — useful for landscape calendars or flip-style notebooks.

Common Issues

Pages out of order: most devices reverse the order automatically for manual duplex; turn that off if your device flips the second side correctly. Inkjet smudges: let the first side dry for 30 seconds before re-feeding.

Set as Default

In the device queue settings, set duplex as the default. Every job will print double-sided unless you override it in the print dialog.

Bottom Line

If you print more than a ream a year, an auto-duplex device pays for the upgrade in paper savings within a year.
