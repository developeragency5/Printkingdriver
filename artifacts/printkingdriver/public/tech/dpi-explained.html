Print Technology

DPI Explained

A clear explanation of dpi explained for non-engineers.

Quick Summary

DPI (dots per inch) measures how many individual dots of ink a device places per inch of paper. Higher DPI produces sharper edges, finer detail, and smoother gradients in photos. For most text documents, 300–600 DPI is sufficient. Photo printing typically benefits from 1200–4800 DPI, depending on the device and paper type.

  • DPI describes output resolution — more dots per inch means finer detail and smoother gradients
  • 300 DPI is the standard for professional-quality text and most business graphics
  • Photo printing at 1200 DPI or higher is only necessary when printing at large sizes or on high-gloss photo paper

Overview

DPI (dots per inch) is the resolution of a print or scan. Higher DPI means finer detail but more ink, slower printing and bigger files. Knowing what DPI to choose for what use saves time and money.

What DPI Measures

DPI counts the number of ink or toner dots per linear inch. 300 DPI means 300 × 300 = 90,000 dots per square inch. The higher the DPI, the smoother the gradients and finer the text, but each dot costs ink and time.

Print Use Cases

Plain office documents: 300 DPI is plenty. Photos: 600 DPI looks visibly better than 300. Fine art prints: 1200 DPI is the standard. Anything above 1200 is rarely visible to the naked eye.

Scan Use Cases

Plain text: 200 DPI for OCR, 300 DPI for archival. Photos: 300 DPI for screen, 600 DPI for printing or archival. Old slides and negatives: 1200 DPI to capture all detail.

File Size Impact

Doubling the DPI quadruples the file size. A 300 DPI A4 colour scan is around 25 MB; the same scan at 600 DPI is around 100 MB.

DPI vs PPI

DPI is for devices and scanners; PPI (pixels per inch) is for screens. They are often used interchangeably but they refer to different physical things.

Bottom Line

For everyday work, 300 DPI is the right default. Going higher only matters when the output will be looked at very closely.
